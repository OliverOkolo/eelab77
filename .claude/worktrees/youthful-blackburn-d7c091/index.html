Features

Not a toy. A real instrument.

Built with the same features you'd expect from a bench scope — just running in a browser tab.

📡
Plug and play via Web Serial

Chrome's Web Serial API talks directly to the Arduino over USB. No drivers, no middleware, no server. Flash once, open the page, click Connect.

⚡
Stable triggered capture

A proper hardware-style trigger state machine — armed, triggered, holdoff. Waveforms lock solid on screen instead of drifting.

📊
2-channel with independent controls

Sample A0 and A1 simultaneously. Each channel has its own V/div, offset, AC coupling, invert, and colour. CH2 trigger support included.

🔢
FFT frequency spectrum

Live Fast Fourier Transform with a Hann window and dBV scale. Split-screen view alongside the time-domain trace, for both channels.

📐
Cursors with ΔT / ΔV readout

Drag time and voltage cursor lines on the canvas. A readout panel shows T1, T2, ΔT, 1/ΔT (frequency), V1, V2, and ΔV in real time.

🧮
Average and peak detect

Stack 2–64 captures and average them to cut noise. Peak detect holds the element-wise envelope across all captures to expose glitches.

📤
Export PNG and CSV

Save the current waveform as a PNG screenshot, or as a CSV with time, raw ADC, and voltage columns — ready to open in MATLAB, Python, or Excel.

🩺
Built-in diagnostics

Live bytes/sec, frame health percentage, sync error count, and a serial echo test that reads ADC values from the Arduino directly into the browser.

🔧
AC coupling and invert

AC coupling subtracts the DC component to centre the signal on screen. Invert flips the trace. Both work on top of any acquisition mode.

Setup

Three steps. That's it.

Getting started takes about five minutes from a cold start.

01

Flash the sketch

Click "Get Sketch" in the scope, copy the code into Arduino IDE, and upload it to your Arduino. Takes about two minutes.

02

Wire your signal

Connect your signal to A0 for CH1 and A1 for CH2. Signal must be 0–5 V GND-referenced. Use a voltage divider for protection on real-world signals.

03

Open and connect

Open the scope in Chrome or Edge, select your baud rate, click Connect, and choose the COM port. The waveform appears immediately.

Protect your Arduino

The Arduino's ADC input tolerates 0–5 V only. For real-world signals, add a simple protection circuit: a 10kΩ series resistor and two 1N4148 diodes clamping to GND and 5V. This prevents the ADC from being damaged by overvoltage spikes.
